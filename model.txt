==============================================================================
  AGRISIGNAL — MODEL MATH & REASONING GUIDE
==============================================================================

This file explains every model and algorithm in the project from first
principles. No machine learning background assumed.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  1. THE CORE BUSINESS INSIGHT
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Natural gas (NG) accounts for 70–90% of the cost to produce ammonia, which
is then converted to urea (a nitrogen fertilizer). The production chain is:

    Natural Gas  →  Ammonia (Haber-Bosch process)  →  Urea

Because this conversion takes time and is subject to plant scheduling, changes
in the NG spot price tend to show up in fertilizer prices 4–8 weeks later.
This lag is the entire basis for forecasting: if we watch NG prices move
today, we can estimate where urea prices are heading next month or the month
after.

Henry Hub (Louisiana) is the U.S. benchmark NG price, reported in $/MMBtu
(dollars per million British thermal units). World Bank Pink Sheet reports
urea in $/mt (dollars per metric ton). Both are monthly series.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  2. DATA PIPELINE
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

All data is aligned to a monthly DatetimeIndex (month-start format).

  Source 1: Henry Hub monthly spot price
    File: data/natural-gas-prices/monthly.csv
    Unit: $/MMBtu
    Coverage: 1997–2025

  Source 2: World Bank fertilizer prices
    File: data/raw/CMO-Historical-Data-Monthly.xlsx
    Columns used: Urea ($/mt), DAP — diammonium phosphate ($/mt)
    Coverage: 1960–present, but we use 2018–2024 for training

  Source 3: EIA underground storage
    File: data/series_data/NG_SUM_LSUM_DCU_NUS_M.xls
    Unit: MMcf (million cubic feet)
    Why: Low storage → tight supply → upward price pressure on NG → leads
         to higher fertilizer prices downstream

The pipeline clips everything to 2018-01 → 2024-12 for model training.
This gives 84 monthly observations — a small but clean dataset.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  3. FEATURE ENGINEERING (backend/src/features/engineer.py)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Starting from 4 raw series (ng_spot, urea, dap, storage_mmcf), we derive
21 additional columns. Here is what each one measures and why it matters.

  A. LAG FEATURES  —  ng_lag1 through ng_lag4

    ng_lag1 = ng_spot shifted back 1 month
    ng_lag2 = ng_spot shifted back 2 months
    ... etc.

    Why: The model needs to "see" recent NG history to detect trends. A lag-1
    of the most recent month, a lag-2 of two months ago, etc. give the model
    a short time window without needing a recurrent neural network.

    Formally: ng_lag_k[t] = ng_spot[t - k]


  B. ROLLING MEANS  —  ng_rolling_mean_3m, ng_rolling_mean_6m

    ng_rolling_mean_3m[t] = mean(ng_spot[t-2], ng_spot[t-1], ng_spot[t])

    Why: Smooths out short-term noise. A 3-month window captures the
    "recent trend" while a 6-month window captures the "medium-term regime."
    If both rolling means are rising, that is a stronger signal than just
    the last data point being high.


  C. ROLLING STANDARD DEVIATIONS  —  ng_rolling_std_3m, ng_rolling_std_6m

    ng_rolling_std_3m[t] = std(ng_spot[t-2], ng_spot[t-1], ng_spot[t])

    Why: Measures recent price volatility. High std means NG is moving
    erratically — which increases forecast uncertainty. The model can learn
    that a volatile NG environment leads to wider urea price swings.


  D. MOMENTUM  —  ng_mom_1m, ng_mom_3m, ng_mom_6m

    ng_mom_1m[t] = (ng_spot[t] - ng_spot[t-1]) / ng_spot[t-1]

    This is the percent change over 1, 3, and 6 months. Unlike lags (which
    give raw price levels), momentum gives the rate of change. The model
    can use both: "NG is at $3 and rising 20% over 3 months" is different
    from "NG is at $3 and flat."


  E. UREA FEATURES  —  urea_lag1, urea_rolling_mean_3m, urea_ng_ratio

    urea_lag1 = urea[t-1]  (last month's price)
    urea_rolling_mean_3m = mean of last 3 months of urea
    urea_ng_ratio = urea[t] / ng_spot[t]

    The ratio is especially powerful. Historically, urea trades at roughly
    5–10× the price of natural gas (in $/MMBtu vs $/mt). When the ratio
    is unusually low, urea is "cheap relative to its feedstock" and is
    likely to revert upward. When the ratio is high, urea may be
    overpriced and could fall.


  F. DAP LAG  —  dap_lag1

    DAP (diammonium phosphate) is a different fertilizer but shares
    production inputs and demand cycles with urea. Including last month's
    DAP price adds a correlated commodity signal the model can leverage.


  G. STORAGE Z-SCORE  —  storage_zscore

    storage_zscore[t] = (storage_mmcf[t] - mean_storage) / std_storage

    A z-score expresses how far the current value is from the historical
    average in units of standard deviation. A z-score of -2 means storage
    is 2 standard deviations below average — extremely tight supply.

    Low storage → supply scarcity → NG prices rise → fertilizer prices rise.
    This gives the model a leading fundamental indicator.


  H. SEASON DUMMIES  —  season_q1, season_q2, season_q3, season_q4

    Binary 0/1 flags for which calendar quarter we are in.
    Q1 = Jan/Feb/Mar, Q2 = Apr/May/Jun, etc.

    Why: Fertilizer demand is seasonal. Spring (Q2) sees heavy application
    for planting; fall (Q4) sees pre-purchase before winter. Prices respond
    to this demand cycle. Dummies let the model learn "Q2 is typically
    expensive" without needing any other indicator.


  I. TARGETS  —  target_urea_t1, target_urea_t2, target_urea_t3

    target_urea_t1[t] = urea[t+1]   (actual price NEXT month)
    target_urea_t2[t] = urea[t+2]   (actual price in 2 months)
    target_urea_t3[t] = urea[t+3]   (actual price in 3 months)

    These are what each model is trained to predict. The targets are formed
    by shifting the urea series FORWARD (negative shift). The last 3 rows
    of the feature store will have NaN targets — those are the rows we use
    for out-of-sample prediction (the future).

    IMPORTANT: Targets are NEVER used as input features. Using future urea
    prices as features would be data leakage — training on information the
    model cannot have at prediction time.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  4. XGBOOST FERTILIZER PRICE FORECASTER
     (backend/src/models/forecaster.py)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We train THREE separate XGBoost Regressor models — one per horizon:
  t1 model: predicts urea price 1 month ahead
  t2 model: predicts urea price 2 months ahead
  t3 model: predicts urea price 3 months ahead

Each model is completely independent. We treat the multi-step forecast as
three separate single-step problems rather than a recursive chain. This
prevents error accumulation (recursive forecasting would feed t1 predictions
back in to generate t2, compounding errors).

  WHAT IS XGBOOST?

  XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting) builds an ensemble of decision trees
  sequentially. Each tree is trained to correct the residual errors of all
  previous trees. The final prediction is the sum of all tree outputs.

  Mathematically:
    F_0(x) = base prediction (mean of y)
    F_m(x) = F_{m-1}(x) + learning_rate * h_m(x)
           where h_m is the m-th tree trained on residuals of F_{m-1}

  For our problem, x is the 21-feature vector (NG lags, momentum, etc.)
  and y is the future urea price.

  KEY HYPERPARAMETERS (and why we chose them):

    n_estimators = 300      Number of trees. More trees = better fit but
                            slower. 300 is a good balance for 84 data points.

    max_depth = 4           Maximum depth of each tree. Shallow trees (3–4)
                            prevent overfitting on our small dataset. A tree
                            of depth 4 can capture interactions between up to
                            4 features at once.

    learning_rate = 0.05    Each tree's contribution is shrunk by this factor.
                            Small learning rate + many trees = better
                            generalization than large rate + few trees.

    subsample = 0.8         Each tree is trained on a random 80% sample of
                            rows. Reduces correlation between trees.

    colsample_bytree = 0.8  Each tree uses a random 80% of features.
                            Also reduces overfitting.

    min_child_weight = 3    A leaf node must contain at least 3 observations.
                            Prevents fitting noise in outlier months.

    objective = reg:squarederror  We minimize mean squared error (MSE).
                            Loss = (1/n) * sum( (y_pred - y_true)^2 )


  DATA SPLITS:

    Training + Validation: 2018-01 → 2022-12  (60 months)
    Test holdout:          2023-01 → 2024-12  (24 months, never touched during tuning)

  The 2022 price spike (Russia/Ukraine war caused NG and fertilizer to
  spike 3-5×) is deliberately included in training so the model has seen
  extreme volatility.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  5. WALK-FORWARD CROSS-VALIDATION
     (backend/src/models/forecaster.py — TimeSeriesSplit)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Standard k-fold cross-validation randomly shuffles data before splitting.
This is WRONG for time series because it allows the model to "see the future"
during training (e.g., train on 2022 data, validate on 2019 data).

Walk-forward CV respects the arrow of time:

    Fold 1: Train on rows 1–12,  validate on rows 13–20
    Fold 2: Train on rows 1–20,  validate on rows 21–28
    Fold 3: Train on rows 1–28,  validate on rows 29–36
    Fold 4: Train on rows 1–36,  validate on rows 37–44
    Fold 5: Train on rows 1–44,  validate on rows 45–52

  gap=1 means there is a 1-row gap between train and validation to prevent
  boundary leakage (the model cannot use the month immediately before
  prediction to "peek").

  WHY THIS MATTERS FOR MONTE CARLO:

  On each validation fold, we collect residuals:
    residual[i] = y_true[i] - y_pred[i]

  Across all folds we get a distribution of residuals. The standard
  deviation of this distribution (residual_std) measures how wrong the
  model typically is. This is saved to model_metadata.json and used
  as the "uncertainty budget" for Monte Carlo simulation.

  Our residual_std is ~$247/mt, which is large — driven by the 2022 spike.
  The test RMSE on 2023–2024 is only ~$91/mt, meaning the model performs
  much better on "normal" markets.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  6. MONTE CARLO SIMULATION
     (backend/src/simulation/monte_carlo.py)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Monte Carlo simulation answers: "Given that our point forecast could be
wrong by some amount, what is the full range of likely outcomes?"

  ALGORITHM (vectorized, 10,000 paths):

    For each horizon h in {t1, t2, t3}:
      1. Draw 10,000 errors from a Normal distribution:
           epsilon_i ~ N(residual_mean_h, residual_std_h)
      2. Add each error to the XGBoost point forecast:
           sim_price_i = point_forecast_h + epsilon_i
      3. Clip to physically plausible range [100, 2000] $/mt
           (urea has never been below $100 or above $2,000 in history)

  The Normal distribution assumption says: "Errors are symmetric and bell-
  shaped around zero (or near-zero mean)." This is a simplification — real
  residuals can be skewed — but it works well for a first approximation.

  KEY OUTPUTS:

    p10_t2 = 10th percentile of sim prices at t2 → optimistic scenario
    p50_t2 = 50th percentile (median) → best single estimate
    p90_t2 = 90th percentile → pessimistic scenario
    p10–p90 together form the "80% confidence interval"

    prob_rising_t2 = fraction of 1,000 paths where sim_t2 > current_price
                   = P(urea is higher in 60 days than it is today)

    prob_10pct_increase_t2 = fraction where sim_t2 > current * 1.10

  The t2 distribution (1,000 sampled values) is stored in cache.json
  for the histogram chart in the Streamlit dashboard.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  7. BUY SIGNAL ENGINE
     (backend/src/signals/engine.py)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The signal engine translates the numeric forecast output into a plain-English
recommendation for farmers. Four possible signals, decided by two numbers:

    pct_change_t2 = (forecast_t2 - current_urea) / current_urea
                  = expected % price change over 60 days

    prob_rising   = from Monte Carlo (see above)

  DECISION TABLE:

    BUY_NOW          pct_change_t2 > 8%  AND prob_rising > 65%
                     → Prices strongly forecast to rise; buy soon to lock
                       in current lower price.

    CONSIDER_BUYING  pct_change_t2 > 4%  OR  prob_rising > 55%
                     → Prices lean upward but signal is not definitive.
                       Partial pre-purchase may be sensible.

    WAIT             pct_change_t2 < -4% AND prob_rising < 40%
                     → Prices forecast to fall; waiting saves money.

    NEUTRAL          everything else
                     → No clear signal; prices expected to be stable.

  The thresholds (8%, 4%, 65%, 55%) were set per the project spec based on
  typical urea price volatility. In a real product these would be calibrated
  against historical signal performance.

  CONFIDENCE is defined as:
    prob_rising              if signal is BUY_NOW or CONSIDER_BUYING
    1 - prob_rising          if signal is WAIT
    (measures how sure we are in the direction of the recommendation)

  BEST MONTH TO BUY = the horizon (t1, t2, t3) with the lowest p50 forecast.
  A rising forecast means buying at t1 is cheapest. A falling forecast means
  waiting until t3 saves the most money.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  8. EXPOSURE CALCULATOR
     (implemented in dashboard.py and backend/server.js)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Converts the abstract $/mt forecast into a concrete dollar cost for the
specific farmer. Math:

    lbs_nitrogen_needed = acreage × N_intensity_lbs_per_acre

    urea_metric_tons = lbs_nitrogen_needed / (0.46 × 2204.6)

  Where:
    0.46 = urea is 46% nitrogen by weight (the rest is inert filler)
    2204.6 = pounds per metric ton (lbs/mt)

  Nitrogen intensity by crop (lbs N per acre):
    Corn:     150  (heavy feeder, applied pre-plant and side-dress)
    Wheat:    90   (moderate; fall application for winter wheat)
    Cotton:   120  (high-value crop, heavy inputs)
    Sorghum:  80   (drought tolerant, lower input intensity)
    Soybeans: 60   (fixes atmospheric N, so lower synthetic requirement)
    Hay:      50   (grass hay; legume hay may need even less)

  Then:
    cost_at_t2_expected = urea_metric_tons × forecast_t2_p50
    cost_at_t2_low      = urea_metric_tons × forecast_t2_p10
    cost_at_t2_high     = urea_metric_tons × forecast_t2_p90

  The "pre-purchased %" slider reduces urea_metric_tons proportionally:
    remaining_mt = total_mt × (1 - pre_purchased_pct / 100)


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  9. RANDOM FOREST FARM RISK MODEL
     (backend/training_and_eval.py, dashboard.py)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This is a separate, independent model from the XGBoost forecaster. It does
not predict fertilizer prices. Instead it predicts financial stress risk
for individual farm borrowers.

  WHAT IS A RANDOM FOREST?

  A Random Forest is an ensemble of decision trees trained on random
  subsets of the data (bootstrapped rows) and random subsets of features.
  The final prediction is the average (for regression) or majority vote
  (for classification) across all trees.

    Bootstrap sample: train tree i on a random sample WITH replacement
    Feature subset: each split considers only sqrt(num_features) features

  This randomness makes trees decorrelated. Averaging uncorrelated trees
  reduces variance without increasing bias — this is the core statistical
  insight behind ensemble methods.

  OUR SETUP:

    n_estimators = 100       100 trees in the forest
    max_depth = 5            Prevents each tree from memorizing training data
    class_weight = "balanced" Auto-adjusts for imbalanced classes
                             (we have ~20% high-risk, 80% healthy)

  INPUT FEATURES:

    Numerical:
      Farm_Area_Acres         — larger farms may have more leverage
      Fertilizer_Used_Tons    — proxy for input cost exposure
      Current_LTV_Ratio       — loan-to-value (higher = more leveraged)
      Months_Since_Delinquency — payment history signal (-1 = never late)

    Categorical (OneHotEncoded):
      Crop_Type     — different crops have different risk profiles
      Irrigation_Type — drip/sprinkler more capital intensive than rain-fed
      Soil_Type      — affects yield reliability
      Season         — Kharif (monsoon), Rabi (winter), Zaid (summer)

  TARGET:

    Requires_Intervention = 1 if Stress_Probability > 0.65 else 0

    Stress_Probability was synthetically generated as:
      stress = f(LTV, delinquency, crop risk, weather exposure)

    This is a BINARY classification problem (not regression).
    The model outputs a probability P(Requires_Intervention = 1) via
    predict_proba().

  EVALUATION METRICS:

    Confusion Matrix tells you the four possible outcomes:
      True Negative  (TN): healthy farm, model says healthy
      False Positive (FP): healthy farm, model flags as risky (wasteful)
      False Negative (FN): risky farm, model misses it (dangerous)
      True Positive  (TP): risky farm, model correctly flags it

    Precision  = TP / (TP + FP)   — of all flagged, how many were truly risky?
    Recall     = TP / (TP + FN)   — of all risky, how many did we catch?
    F1         = 2 × (P × R) / (P + R) — harmonic mean, balances both

    ROC-AUC: plots True Positive Rate vs False Positive Rate at every
    possible threshold. AUC = 0.857 means that if you randomly pick one
    risky farm and one healthy farm, the model will correctly rank the
    risky one as higher risk 85.7% of the time. Anything above 0.8 is
    considered good.

  Our model: 80% accuracy, 0.857 ROC-AUC, 61% recall on high-risk class.
  On synthetic data this is strong. On real farm data you would expect lower
  performance and would need to calibrate thresholds based on the cost of
  false negatives vs false positives.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  10. PRIORITY SCORE (Risk × Fertilizer Overlay)
      (dashboard.py — Portfolio Risk tab)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

To help loan officers prioritize outreach, we combine BOTH models:

    Priority_Score = High_Risk_Pct × (1 + max(fc_pct, 0) / 100)

  Where:
    High_Risk_Pct = % of borrowers in this crop segment flagged by RF model
    fc_pct        = expected % fertilizer price increase over 60 days (XGBoost)
    max(..., 0)   = only penalize for rising prices, not falling ones
                    (a price drop doesn't make existing risk worse)

  Example:
    Corn borrowers: 30% high risk, fertilizer forecast +20%
    Priority = 30 × (1 + 0.20) = 36.0

    Wheat borrowers: 25% high risk, fertilizer forecast +20%
    Priority = 25 × (1 + 0.20) = 30.0

  Corn gets contacted first because more of those borrowers are already
  struggling AND their input costs are about to rise the most.

  This is a simple but interpretable heuristic. A more rigorous version
  would use expected loss = P(default) × exposure at default, adjusted
  for the expected cost increase, which would require actual loan balances.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  11. KNOWN LIMITATIONS & HONEST CAVEATS
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  a) SMALL DATASET: 84 monthly observations is very small for ML. XGBoost
     with 300 trees on 84 rows can overfit easily. max_depth=4 and
     min_child_weight=3 help but do not eliminate this risk.

  b) DIRECTIONAL ACCURACY: Our t2 model correctly predicts the direction of
     price movement only ~24% of the time in testing — worse than random.
     This is driven by the 2022 spike being in validation folds during CV.
     The test RMSE ($91/mt) is more informative for this dataset.

  c) WIDE MC BANDS: The Monte Carlo bands ($135–$808 on a $352 price) are
     too wide because residual_std ($247) was calculated on CV folds that
     included the 2022 spike. A more refined approach would use recent
     residuals only or a regime-aware model.

  d) SYNTHETIC FARM DATA: The farm risk model was trained on 1,000 rows of
     synthetically generated data. Real-world performance would require
     actual loan book data with verified outcomes.

  e) STALE FORECAST: cache.json is generated once by run_pipeline.py and
     served indefinitely. The dashboard will show stale forecasts until you
     re-run the pipeline. The sidebar shows the generation timestamp.

  f) NO FUTURES DATA: The EIA_API_KEY in .env could pull NG futures for a
     forward-looking market signal, but this is not yet implemented.
